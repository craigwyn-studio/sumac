SECTION 1 — Welcome to the Application Simulation

You’ve spent the last six weeks learning how mathematicians think:

  • how definitions drive proofs,
  • how structure replaces computation,

Up to now, each week had:

This week removes some of that scaffolding.

Just like the real SUMaC application:

SECTION 3 — What SUMaC Is Really Evaluating This Week

SUMaC is not  checking whether you know advanced math.

They are looking for evidence of mathematical maturity :

  • Can you start from clear definitions?
  • Can you organize a solution logically?
  • Can you explain why  something must be true?
  • Can you communicate your ideas cleanly to a reader?
  • Can you make progress even when the path isn’t obvious?

A partial but well-explained solution is often more valuable  than a rushed or unclear complete one.

SECTION 4 — Expectations for the Simulation

Treat this week as if it were the real application:

  • Work carefully, not quickly.
  • Write full sentences where explanation matters.
  • Avoid excessive computation.
  • Use small examples only to inform , not replace, reasoning.
  • If you get stuck, explain what you tried and why.

That’s exactly what mathematicians do — and exactly what SUMaC respects.

SECTION 5 — How to Approach Each Problem

Before writing anything, pause and ask:

  1. What is being claimed?
  2. What definitions are hiding inside the words?
  3. What structure might simplify the problem?
  4. Which proof styles could work here?

  • direct?
  • contradiction?
  • modular reasoning?
  • invariants?
  • case analysis?

Then choose one  path and commit to explaining it clearly.

Clarity beats cleverness every time.

SECTION 6 — A Reassuring Truth

No one expects perfection.

Every strong mathematician:

  • hesitates,
  • revises,
  • restarts,
  • and improves their explanation.

What matters is that your thinking is honest, structured, and readable .
