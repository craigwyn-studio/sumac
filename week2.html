PROOF-WRITING RUBRIC

A. Structure (0–4)

  • Clear beginning, middle, and end to the argument.
  • Logical sequencing of steps; each idea follows naturally from the previous one.

B. Rigor & Correctness (0–4)

  • Every step is justified (algebraically, geometrically, or via a cited theorem).
  • No gaps or unjustified leaps in logic.
  • Final conclusion correctly answers the prompt.

C. Clarity (0–4)

  • Sentences are readable and coherent.
  • Notation is consistent and standard.
  • Explanations accompany symbolic steps, not only computations.

D. Insight / Strategy (0–4)

  • Choice of method is appropriate and efficient.
  • Solution demonstrates mathematical insight.

Problem 1 — Induction Inequality

What this is about: Mathematical induction is a method for proving statements that hold for all integers above a starting point. Think of it as a domino effect: if the first falls (base case) and each domino pushes the next, then all fall. Here, we’re proving an inequality involving exponential growth.

Why it matters: Induction underlies proofs across number theory, combinatorics, and computer science. The skill of turning “the n-case → the (n+1)-case” is foundational.

What the problem is really asking: Show that an exponential term 2n stays ahead of (or outpaces) a quadratic-like expression n2 + n + 1. This blends algebraic manipulation with the structural logic of induction.

The approachable idea: The key is to look at the difference between the two sides. When going from n to n+1, exponentials multiply by 2 — quadratics only grow by about 2n + 3. Understanding this growth rate difference makes the induction step transparent.

Your task: Prove by induction that 2n ≥ n2 + n + 1 for all integers n ≥ 5.

Problem 2 — Modular Squares (Number Theory)

What this is about: Modular arithmetic is like clock math: numbers “wrap around” after a certain modulus. Here, we study the behavior of perfect squares modulo 3.

Why it matters: Modular reasoning appears constantly in number theory — from primality tests to cryptography. Understanding the behavior of squares mod small numbers builds intuition for larger structures.

What the problem is really asking: Determine all possible remainders a square can have upon division by 3, and then use that fact to solve a structural number-theory question.

The approachable idea: There are only three possible values mod 3: 0, 1, 2. Compute squares of representatives and classify them. This leads to a strong restriction: square mod 3 can only be 0 or 1.

Your task:

  • Show that if n is an integer, then n2 ≡ 0 or 1 (mod 3).
  • Then use this to deduce that no integer of the form 3k + 2 can be a perfect square.

Problem 3 — Counting Seating Arrangements (Combinatorics)

What this is about: Arrangements and permutations are fundamental in combinatorics. This problem asks you to count how many ways to seat people under certain color constraints.

Why it matters: Constraints transform simple counting into structured combinatorial reasoning. These ideas appear in probability, computer science, and algorithms.

What the problem is really asking: Seat three green-shirt students first, then choose two specific seats for students wearing blue shirts. The sequence matters — position constraints limit choices.

The approachable idea: Break the problem into sequential decisions: (1) Fix the greens in order. (2) Choose seats for blues. (3) Multiply the independent choices.

Your task:

  • You have 5 seats in a row.
  • Seats 1–3 must be filled by three green-shirt students (in a fixed order: G1, G2, G3).
  • Seats 4–5 must be filled by two blue-shirt students (but choose which blue student goes where).
  • Count the total number of valid arrangements.

Problem 4 — Functional Equation

What this is about: A functional equation restricts how a function behaves based on relationships between its inputs. This one involves evaluating the function at scaled arguments.

Why it matters: Functional equations are common in Olympiad problems, physics (symmetry), and calculus (defining the exponential, etc.). Understanding how to “probe” the equation with clever substitutions is critical.

What the problem is really asking: Find all real-valued functions f satisfying f(2x) = 4f(x) + 2x.

The approachable idea: Try evaluating the function at specific values (like x = 0) to find structural constraints. The equation resembles behavior of quadratics: doubling the input multiplies some terms by 4, which hints at an x2 component.

Your task: Solve the functional equation fully — determine all real functions f: ℝ → ℝ satisfying f(2x) = 4f(x) + 2x.

Problem 5 — Ramsey Triangle (Graph Theory / Combinatorics)

What this is about: This is a classic small case of Ramsey theory — the idea that complete disorder is impossible when a structure grows large enough. With only 6 people, structure must emerge.

Why it matters: Ramsey theory underpins graph theory, communication networks, logic, and many areas of pure math. The “friend/stranger” dichotomy is the foundational example.

What the problem is really asking: You color each edge of a 6-person graph either red (friends) or blue (strangers). Show there must be a monochromatic triangle: three mutual friends or three mutual strangers.

The approachable idea: Use the pigeonhole principle: pick one person. They are connected to 5 others. At least 3 edges must be the same color. Then analyze those 3 neighbors.

Your task: Prove that in any 2-coloring of the edges of K6 (the complete graph on 6 vertices), there is a monochromatic triangle.

SOLUTIONS — WEEK 2

Solution 1 — Induction Inequality

(a) Base case. Check n = 5: 25 = 32 and 52 + 5 + 1 = 31, so 25 ≥ 52 + 5 + 1.

(b) Induction step. Assume 2n ≥ n2 + n + 1 for some n ≥ 5. Multiply both sides by 2:

2n+1 ≥ 2n2 + 2n + 2.

We want to show 2n+1 ≥ (n+1)2 + (n+1) + 1 = n2 + 3n + 3.

So it suffices to prove 2n2 + 2n + 2 ≥ n2 + 3n + 3, i.e. n2 − n − 1 ≥ 0.

The quadratic n2 − n − 1 is positive for all n ≥ 2, so the induction step holds. Thus 2n ≥ n2 + n + 1 for all n ≥ 5.

Solution 2 — Modular Squares

Consider n modulo 3. Every integer is congruent to 0, 1, or 2 (mod 3).

  • If n ≡ 0 (mod 3), then n2 ≡ 0 (mod 3).
  • If n ≡ 1 (mod 3), then n2 ≡ 1 (mod 3).
  • If n ≡ 2 (mod 3), then n2 ≡ 4 ≡ 1 (mod 3).

Thus n2 ≡ 0 or 1 (mod 3). In particular, n2 can never be ≡ 2 (mod 3), so numbers of the form 3k + 2 are not perfect squares.

Solution 3 — Counting Seating Arrangements

Seats 1–3 are fixed as G1, G2, G3 in that order. Seats 4–5 must be filled by two blue-shirt students B1 and B2.

There are 2! = 2 ways to permute B1 and B2 in seats 4 and 5. Thus there are exactly 2 valid arrangements.

Solution 4 — Functional Equation

We seek all real-valued functions f satisfying f(2x) = 4f(x) + 2x.

Assume f is a polynomial of degree at most 2 and try f(x) = ax2 + bx + c.

Then f(2x) = 4ax2 + 2bx + c, while 4f(x) + 2x = 4(ax2 + bx + c) + 2x = 4ax2 + 4bx + 4c + 2x.

Equating coefficients gives:

  • For x2: 4a = 4a (always true).
  • For x: 2b = 4b + 2 ⇒ −2b = 2 ⇒ b = −1.
  • For constants: c = 4c ⇒ −3c = 0 ⇒ c = 0.

So f(x) = ax2 − x for some real a. Substituting back confirms these all satisfy the equation. Hence all solutions are f(x) = ax2 − x, with a any real constant.

Solution 5 — Ramsey Triangle

Model the situation as a complete graph K6 whose vertices are the six people. Color an edge red if two people are friends and blue if they are strangers.

Pick any person P. P is connected to 5 others, so among the 5 edges from P at least 3 have the same color by the pigeonhole principle.

Assume at least 3 of these edges are red, connecting P to A, B, and C. Consider the triangle formed by A, B, and C.

If any of AB, BC, or CA is red, then P together with that red edge forms a red triangle. If none of AB, BC, CA is red, then all three are blue, giving a blue triangle ABC.

In either case, there is a monochromatic triangle. Thus any 2-coloring of the edges of K6 contains a monochromatic triangle.
