A Friendly Guide to Your 7-Week Journey into Proof-Based Mathematics
(Tone D: warm, welcoming, clear, lightly academic)

1. Welcome Letter

Dear SUMaC Applicant,

Welcome to this 7-week guided introduction to the style of mathematics that SUMaC (Stanford University Mathematics Camp) loves: proof-based reasoning, patterns, number theory, and creative problem solving.

This program is designed for students who want to strengthen the exact skills SUMaC looks for — gently, clearly, and without pressure.

You do not need to be an expert in number theory.
You do not need to be an olympiad competitor.
And you do not need years of proof experience.

Most SUMaC students start where you are: strong in math, curious, motivated — but new to formal proofs.

This 7-week program will help you feel confident, capable, and fully prepared to approach the SUMaC application problems with clarity and excitement.

You are absolutely capable of this journey. Let’s begin it together step by step.

2. What SUMaC Really Expects (In Plain, Friendly Terms)

SUMaC is not looking for students who already know high-level mathematics such as:

  • abstract algebra
  • advanced number theory
  • analysis
  • cryptography
  • or Olympiad-level topics

SUMaC is looking for students who:

  • think clearly
  • write their reasoning step by step
  • can follow logical arguments
  • can explain why something is true
  • enjoy patterns and curious questions
  • are willing to explore a new style of math

In other words: SUMaC cares more about mathematical maturity than about prior knowledge.

The application problem set each year is short — usually only 3 to 5 problems. But the solutions require clear structure, reasoning, creativity, and a bit of patience.

This 7-week program is designed to help you build exactly that.

3. What You Do Not Need to Know (and Why)

This is important.

SUMaC does not expect you to know:

  • deep number theory theorems
  • advanced combinatorics
  • ring theory
  • modular inverses beyond basic concepts
  • cryptographic algorithms
  • contest-level shortcuts
  • complex proofs you’ve never seen

Why?

Because SUMaC is meant to teach those things.

What you need is simply:

  • the ability to read and write basic proofs
  • comfort with small steps and patterns
  • a willingness to explore unfamiliar ideas
  • algebraic fluency
  • careful thinking

This program focuses only on the essentials. Everything else is optional.

4. The 7-Week Prep Roadmap

Light, friendly, and designed to fit your life

Each week: ~2 hours total.
Just enough to grow, not enough to overwhelm.

WEEK 0: Welcome Packet + Syllabus
A warm introduction, expectations, study tips.

WEEK 1: Proof Bootcamp
What proofs are, and how mathematicians think. Direct proofs, contrapositive, contradiction, induction basics. Light modular arithmetic introduction.

WEEK 2: Foundations of Number Theory
Divisibility, remainders, Euclid’s algorithm, modular thinking. Friendly problems with step-by-step guidance.

WEEK 3: Modular Arithmetic Essentials
Clock arithmetic, properties, simple linear congruences, patterns. Clear examples and “why this matters” explanations.

WEEK 4: Counting & Combinatorics
Combinations, binomial coefficients, patterns in Pascal’s triangle. Puzzle-style problems (fun and non-intimidating).

WEEK 5: Induction & Invariants
Domino-style induction, strong induction, simple invariants, parity patterns. Confidence-building week.

WEEK 6: SUMaC-Style Problem Set Training
A gentle introduction to SUMaC application problems: 2 guided problems, 2 independent problems, annotated examples.

WEEK 7: Application Simulation
A friendly mini–SUMaC problem set with full explanations. A confidence check:
“Yes, I can really do this.”

5. The Skills You Will Gain by February

By the end of these 7 weeks, you will be able to:

Proof & Logic

  • Write clear, confident proofs
  • Use direct, contrapositive, and contradiction methods
  • Apply induction to patterns and formulas

Number Theory

  • Understand modular arithmetic (the language of SUMaC Track A)
  • Solve simple modular equations
  • Use Euclid’s algorithm for greatest common divisors
  • Recognize number patterns that appear on SUMaC problems

Combinatorics & Counting

  • Work with binomial coefficients
  • Use counting strategies in puzzles
  • Recognize combinatorial reasoning in proofs

Problem Solving & Communication

  • Break down a complex problem into small steps
  • Test ideas on small cases
  • Write explanations that are clear and readable
  • Structure your solutions like a mathematician

These are exactly the skills SUMaC evaluates.
And you’ll build them gently, week by week.

6. How to Study Each Week

Light weekly habits that work

This program is designed to fit comfortably into your schedule.

Step 1: Read the lesson (30–45 minutes)
These lessons are friendly, clear, and designed not to overwhelm you.

Step 2: Work through guided examples (20–30 minutes)
Examples always show you how to think, not just what to do.

Step 3: Try the problem set (45–60 minutes)
Problems are intentionally selected to build confidence, not stress.

Step 4: Reflect for 3 minutes
Ask yourself:

  • What made sense?
  • What felt confusing?
  • What pattern did I notice?

This reflection builds mathematical maturity faster than anything else.

Step 5: Celebrate small wins
Every new proof is progress.
Every pattern recognized is growth.

7. A Note About Feeling Stuck

(Normal + Expected)

Being “stuck” is not a sign you’re bad at math.
It’s actually the main method mathematicians use to learn.

Real mathematicians:

  • Try examples
  • Make guesses
  • Draw diagrams
  • Get stuck
  • Try again
  • And eventually discover a breakthrough

This is normal. Expected. Healthy. Part of the process.

If you feel stuck during any problem in this program: pause, breathe, and try a tiny case.

Often the answer reveals itself through play and curiosity.
