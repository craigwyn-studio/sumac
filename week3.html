WEEK 3 — Counting & Discrete Structures

A gentle introduction to reasoning by cases, structure, and inevitability

SECTION 1 — Why This Week Exists

Up to now, you’ve been learning how mathematicians reason about numbers.

  • In Week 1, you learned how to explain why something is true.
  • In Week 2, you learned how to find structure inside integers — using divisibility, parity, and remainders.

This week, we shift perspective slightly.

Instead of asking questions like

“What kind of number is this?”

we start asking:

“How many possibilities are there — really?”

This is the beginning of counting arguments and discrete reasoning.

No calculus.
No formulas to memorize.
No advanced combinatorics.

Just clear thinking about finite situations.

SECTION 2 — Discrete Thinking: What Makes This Different?

A discrete problem is one where:

  • things come in separate pieces, not smooth continua
  • you can list possibilities (at least in principle)
  • structure matters more than size

For example:

  • arranging people in seats
  • choosing subsets
  • distributing objects
  • classifying cases
  • showing something must happen

In these problems, brute force usually fails.

What works instead is structure.

SECTION 3 — Counting Is Not About Arithmetic

This is important.

When mathematicians say “counting,” they do not mean:

  • doing long calculations
  • applying complicated formulas
  • grinding through numbers

They mean:

Reasoning about how many possibilities exist — and why.

Very often, the most powerful counting arguments involve almost no computation at all.

Instead, they rely on:

  • careful classification
  • clean case analysis
  • noticing what cannot happen
  • and recognizing when two situations are really the same

This style of thinking appears constantly in SUMaC problems.

SECTION 4 — Reasoning by Cases (Without Losing Control)

One of the core tools this week is case analysis.

At first, this can feel messy:

“What if I miss a case?”

Good news: mathematicians worry about this too.

That’s why good case analysis always begins with a structural reason that the cases are:

  • complete (nothing is missing), and
  • non-overlapping (nothing is counted twice)

For example:

  • parity splits integers into even or odd
  • division by 3 splits integers into three remainder classes
  • positions in a sequence often split into first / last / middle

When cases come from structure, not guessing, they become powerful instead of chaotic.

SECTION 5 — The Pigeonhole Principle (Intuition First)

Here is one of the most famous ideas in discrete mathematics:

If you have more objects than categories,
then at least two objects must share a category.

This is called the Pigeonhole Principle.

It sounds obvious — almost too obvious.

That’s exactly why it’s dangerous.

When applied carefully, it proves results that look surprising, inevitable, or even impossible to avoid.

This week, we will use it in gentle, concrete ways — not as a trick, but as a way of seeing why certain coincidences must occur.

SECTION 6 — Avoiding Overcounting: When “Different” Isn’t Different

Another subtle idea this week is overcounting.

Sometimes we accidentally count the same situation more than once because:

  • order doesn’t matter, but we treated it like it did
  • different descriptions refer to the same outcome
  • symmetry hides equivalence

Good counting arguments are not about “counting faster.”

They’re about:

counting each thing exactly once.

This requires clarity, patience, and honesty — the same skills you’ve been building since Week 1.

SECTION 7 — What You’re Really Learning This Week

Although the problems will look like “counting problems,” the deeper skills are:

  • breaking a situation into exhaustive cases
  • justifying why your cases are complete
  • recognizing hidden structure
  • explaining why something must happen, not just that it does

This is the same style of reasoning used in:

  • combinatorics
  • number theory
  • graph theory
  • algorithm design
  • and many SUMaC application problems

SECTION 8 — A Quiet Reassurance Before You Begin

If counting problems ever make you feel unsure, that’s normal.

They ask you to:

  • slow down
  • be precise
  • think structurally
  • and resist jumping to formulas

That discomfort is a sign of growth.

You are not expected to be fast.
You are not expected to be perfect.

You are expected to think carefully — and you already know how to do that.

Problem 1 — Counting by Structure, Not Computation

What this is about

This problem introduces counting by classification, rather than by direct calculation.

You are not being asked to compute a large number — you are being asked to organize possibilities.

Why it matters

Many SUMaC-style problems rely on counting arguments where the answer is small, but the reasoning must be precise.

Learning to count by structure prevents mistakes and builds confidence.

What the problem is really asking

You are being asked to show that a finite set of possibilities can be broken into clean, non-overlapping cases — and counted without ambiguity.

The approachable idea

Instead of listing everything, ask:

  • What choices are actually independent?
  • Which features determine the outcome?

Solve

How many three-letter strings can be formed using the letters
{A, B, C}
if repetition is allowed?

Explain your reasoning clearly.

Problem 2 — Case Analysis Done Carefully

What this is about

This problem focuses on reasoning by cases — but doing it in a controlled, justified way.

Why it matters

Case analysis is powerful, but only when:

  • the cases are complete, and
  • no case is counted twice

This problem trains you to justify your case split.

What the problem is really asking

You are being asked to show that your cases come from structure, not guesswork.

The approachable idea

Often, the best cases come from:

  • parity (even / odd),
  • position (first / last / middle),
  • or natural constraints in the problem.

Solve

Suppose you choose a number from the set
{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.

Show that either the number is even or it differs by 1 from an even number.

Explain why these cases cover all possibilities.

Problem 3 — When “Different” Isn’t Actually Different

What this is about

This problem introduces the idea of overcounting — when different descriptions refer to the same outcome.

Why it matters

Overcounting is one of the most common errors in counting arguments.

Recognizing symmetry and equivalence is a key mathematical skill.

What the problem is really asking

You are being asked to recognize when order does not matter, even if it looks like it does.

The approachable idea

Ask yourself:

  • Would swapping elements change the outcome?
  • Or would it describe the same situation in a different way?

Solve

You choose two different numbers from the set
{1, 2, 3, 4}.

How many possible choices are there?

Explain why order should not be counted separately.

Problem 4 — A Gentle Pigeonhole Principle

What this is about

This problem introduces the pigeonhole principle in its simplest and most intuitive form.

Why it matters

This principle appears throughout:

  • number theory
  • combinatorics
  • modular reasoning

It proves that certain coincidences are unavoidable.

What the problem is really asking

You are being asked to show that, when possibilities are limited, repetition must occur.

The approachable idea

When there are more objects than categories, at least two objects must share a category.

Solve

Show that among any 5 integers, at least two have the same remainder when divided by 4.

Explain your reasoning without using specific numbers.

Problem 5 — Inevitability Through Counting

What this is about

This problem combines:

  • case analysis
  • counting
  • and inevitability

to show that something must happen, even without knowing exact values.

Why it matters

Many advanced problems don’t ask what happens — they ask why something cannot be avoided.

This is a foundational idea in discrete mathematics.

What the problem is really asking

You are being asked to prove a guaranteed outcome using only the number of possibilities.

The approachable idea

Think carefully about:

  • how many categories exist
  • how many objects are being placed into them

Solve

Show that among any 4 integers, at least two must have the same parity.

Explain why this conclusion does not depend on which integers are chosen.

SECTION 6 — Guided Solutions

Let’s walk through these together, the way a good tutor would — carefully, clearly, and without rushing.

The goal is not just to see that something works, but to understand why it must work.

⭐ Solution 1 — Counting by Structure

Problem:
How many three-letter strings can be formed from {A, B, C}, with repetition allowed?

Step 1 — Understand what is being chosen

A three-letter string has:

  • a first position,
  • a second position,
  • a third position.

Each position is filled independently.

Step 2 — Count choices for each position

For each position, you may choose:

  • A, B, or C.

That is 3 choices per position.

Step 3 — Combine independent choices

Because the choices are independent, we multiply:

3 × 3 × 3 = 27

Final conclusion

There are 27 possible three-letter strings.

✔ The key idea:
We counted by structure (positions), not by listing.

⭐ Solution 2 — Case Analysis with Parity

Problem:
Show that every number in {1,2,3,4,5,6} is either even or differs by 1 from an even number.

Step 1 — Recall the definition

An integer is even if it can be written as:

n = 2k

for some integer k.

Step 2 — Understand what “differs by 1” means

If a number differs by 1 from an even number, it has the form:

n = 2k ± 1

That is, it is odd.

Step 3 — Classify all integers

Every integer is either:

  • even, or
  • odd.

There are no other possibilities.

Step 4 — Apply this to the set

Each number in {1,2,3,4,5,6} must fall into one of these two categories.

Final conclusion

The two cases — “even” or “one away from even” — cover all possibilities.

✔ The key idea:
A valid case split must be complete and non-overlapping.

⭐ Solution 3 — Avoiding Overcounting

Problem:
How many ways can you choose two different numbers from {1,2,3,4}?

Step 1 — Recognize what matters

We are choosing a pair, not an ordered list.

The pair {1,3} is the same as {3,1}.

Step 2 — Count distinct pairs

List the possibilities carefully, without order:

{1,2}
{1,3}
{1,4}
{2,3}
{2,4}
{3,4}

Step 3 — Count them

There are 6 such pairs.

Final conclusion

There are 6 ways to choose two different numbers.

✔ The key idea:
When order doesn’t change the outcome, it must not be counted separately.

⭐ Solution 4 — Pigeonhole Principle (Intro)

Problem:
Show that among any 5 integers, at least two have the same remainder mod 4.

Step 1 — Identify the “categories”

When dividing by 4, possible remainders are:

0, 1, 2, 3

That is 4 categories.

Step 2 — Place the integers into categories

Each of the 5 integers belongs to exactly one remainder category.

Step 3 — Apply the principle

If 5 objects are placed into 4 categories, at least two objects must share a category.

Final conclusion

At least two of the integers have the same remainder when divided by 4.

✔ The key idea:
More objects than categories guarantees repetition.

⭐ Solution 5 — Parity Is Unavoidable

Problem:
Show that among any 4 integers, at least two have the same parity.

Step 1 — Identify the categories

Parity has only two possibilities:

  • even
  • odd

Step 2 — Count objects vs. categories

We are placing 4 integers into 2 categories.

Step 3 — Apply inevitability

With more integers than categories, at least two integers must share the same category.

Final conclusion

At least two integers have the same parity.

✔ The key idea:
Some conclusions follow purely from counting possibilities.
