SECTION 2 — The Core Idea: Tracking What Stays the Same

An invariant  is a quantity or property that remains unchanged , even as a process unfolds.

For example:

  • A game might involve many moves, but something about the position never changes.
  • A transformation might rearrange objects, but preserve a hidden structure.
  • A sequence might grow, but a certain pattern stays fixed.

Invariants help us prove impossibility results :

“No matter how you proceed, you can never reach that outcome.”

This kind of reasoning is both subtle and powerful.

SECTION 3 — Parity as the Simplest Invariant

The most familiar invariant is parity  — whether something is odd or even.

Parity is simple, but don’t underestimate it.

Many complex problems are solved by noticing:

  • an operation preserves parity, or
  • an operation flips parity in a predictable way.

Once you track parity carefully, entire classes of outcomes can be ruled out immediately.

SECTION 4 — Processes, Moves, and States

Invariant arguments often appear in problems that involve:

  • games,
  • repeated moves,
  • transformations,
  • or step-by-step procedures.

These problems can feel confusing at first because there may be many possible sequences of moves.

The key idea is this:

Even if the process is complicated, the invariant is not.

If you can identify what stays the same after every move, you gain control over the entire process.

SECTION 5 — Why Invariants Are So Effective

Invariant-based proofs work because they:

  • ignore unnecessary detail,
  • focus on structure,
  • and replace simulation with logic.

Instead of trying to track every possible outcome, you track one thing  that never changes.

If the final state violates that invariant, then the final state is impossible — no matter how clever the strategy.

SECTION 6 — Constraint-Based Thinking

Invariants are closely related to constraints .

A constraint is a rule that limits what configurations are possible.

Some constraints are obvious.
Others are hidden — and discovering them is the heart of the problem.

This week, you’ll practice learning to ask:

  • “What is forced here?”
  • “What cannot be altered by any move?”
  • “What information survives every transformation?”

Those questions lead directly to invariants.

SECTION 7 — What You’re Really Learning This Week

Although the problems will involve games, numbers, or configurations, the deeper skills are:

  • identifying properties worth tracking,
  • proving that a property truly stays fixed,
  • using that property to rule out outcomes,
  • and explaining impossibility clearly and convincingly.

These ideas appear throughout mathematics:

  • number theory,
  • combinatorics,
  • graph theory,
  • algorithms,
  • and even physics.

Parity (odd vs. even) is the natural thing to track.

Step 2 — Check how each move affects parity

  • Adding 2 to a number does not  change parity.
  • Subtracting 4 also does not  change parity.

Both operations preserve whether the number is odd or even.

Step 3 — Apply the invariant

Since the number starts odd , and every move preserves parity, the number remains odd forever.

Final conclusion

The number can never  become even.

If a property never changes, any final state that violates it is impossible.
