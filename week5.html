WEEK 5 — Functions, Recursion, and Self-Reference

Understanding rules that describe themselves

SECTION 1 — Why This Week Exists

Up to now, you’ve learned how to reason about:

  • statements and proofs (Week 1),
  • structure inside numbers (Week 2),
  • finite possibilities and counting (Week 3),
  • and processes that are controlled by invariants (Week 4).

This week, we shift focus again — not to objects, but to rules .

Instead of asking:

“What is this number?”

we begin asking:

“What rule generates this behavior?”

This leads us into functions , recursion , and self-reference .

SECTION 2 — Functions as Rules, Not Formulas

When students first encounter functions, they often think of them as formulas:

But in proof-based mathematics, a function is not primarily a formula.

It is a rule .

A function answers the question:

“If I give you this input, what output must you produce — and why?”

This perspective is crucial for SUMaC-style problems, where functions are often:

  • defined in words,
  • defined recursively,
  • or defined using their own values.

SECTION 3 — What Does “Recursive” Really Mean?

A recursive definition  describes something in terms of:

  • a starting point, and
  • a rule for building the next step from previous ones.

For example:

  • a sequence defined using earlier terms,
  • a function that refers to its own values,
  • a process that evolves step by step.

Recursion is not about looping blindly.

It is about structure over time .

Once the starting point is fixed, everything else is forced.

SECTION 4 — Why Recursion Feels Hard (and Why That’s Okay)

Recursive problems can feel uncomfortable at first because:

  • you can’t see the entire object at once,
  • the definition unfolds gradually,
  • and you must trust the rule.

That discomfort is normal.

Mathematicians deal with it by:

  • anchoring carefully at the base case,
  • tracking what each step preserves,
  • and reasoning inductively, even when induction is not stated explicitly.

You’ve already practiced these skills — now you’re applying them in a new setting.

SECTION 5 — Functional Equations as Constraints

Sometimes a function is defined not by a formula, but by an equation it must satisfy .

For example:

This kind of statement doesn’t tell you what the function is  —
it tells you what the function must do .

Your task becomes:

  • discovering what rules are compatible with that behavior,
  • and showing that no others are possible.

These problems reward:

  • patience,
  • pattern recognition,
  • and careful substitution.

SECTION 6 — What You’re Really Learning This Week

Although the problems will involve functions and sequences, the deeper ideas are:

  • interpreting definitions precisely,
  • understanding how rules constrain outcomes,
  • reasoning step by step from a base case,
  • and explaining why behavior is forced, not chosen.

This kind of thinking appears everywhere:

  • in mathematics,
  • in computer science,
  • in algorithms,
  • and in systems that evolve over time.

SECTION 7 — A Reassurance Before You Begin

If recursion or functional equations feel abstract at first, that’s normal.

They ask you to:

  • slow down,
  • read definitions carefully,
  • and trust structure over intuition.

You are not expected to “see the answer” immediately.

You are expected to reason carefully — and you already know how to do that.
